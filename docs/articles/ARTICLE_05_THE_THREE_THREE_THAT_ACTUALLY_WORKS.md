# The (3,3) That Actually Works

*Article 5 — The Forge Content Series*
*Target: 900-1100 words | Voice: Technical, historically informed, honest about predecessors*

---

We need to talk about (3,3).

If you were in DeFi in 2021, those four characters either made you money or cost you money. Probably both. OlympusDAO turned a game theory concept into the most viral meme in DeFi history. People added (3,3) to their Twitter handles. They evangelized staking as a moral act. They treated selling as betrayal.

And then the math caught up.

OHM's (3,3) was elegant in theory. The Prisoner's Dilemma applied to staking: if everyone stakes, everyone wins. The matrix was clean, the logic was sound, and thousands of people bought in because it felt like an intellectual framework, not just another yield farm.

The problem was the yield source. OHM's rewards came from token emissions. Rebasing. The protocol printed new OHM and gave it to stakers. As long as new money entered faster than tokens were created, the price held. The moment that flipped, (3,3) became (-3,-3) at terrifying speed. The same social dynamics that drove the upside — peer pressure to stake, shame for selling — accelerated the downside when conviction broke.

Wonderland (TIME) ran the same playbook. Daniele Sestagalli forked OHM, added populist energy and a "Frog Nation" identity. At peak, Frog Nation protocols hit $4 billion combined market cap. Then the 0xSifu scandal hit, trust evaporated, and the protocol unwound in weeks.

The lesson people took from OHM and Wonderland was that (3,3) doesn't work. That game theory applied to staking is inherently Ponzi-like. That the matrix is a trap.

We disagree. The matrix was right. The yield source was wrong.

Here's what the (3,3) matrix looks like in The Forge:

```
                They Stake    They Bet    They Sell
You Stake       (3,3)         (3,1)       (3,-3)
You Bet         (1,3)         (1,1)       (1,-3)
You Sell        (-3,3)        (-3,1)      (-3,-3)
```

Same matrix. Completely different economics underneath.

When someone stakes in The Forge, their yield comes from three sources. Two of them have nothing to do with emissions.

Source one: protocol rake. Every bet placed in every bout generates a 5% rake. Half goes to stakers. This scales with activity. More bouts, more bets, more rake. The yield isn't printed. It's earned from real betting volume.

Source two: rage quit taxes. When someone unstakes, they pay a tax — up to 50% depending on their covenant and timing. That tax goes directly to remaining stakers. This source is anti-fragile in a way that would have saved OHM: when the market panics and people sell, stakers earn more. Not less. More. The exits of the fearful fund the returns of the patient.

Source three: treasury emissions. This is the OHM-like component, and it's deliberately constrained. 15% of the weekly treasury budget, decaying over four years. Year one: 3.85M per week. Year four: 481K per week. By the time emissions become negligible, the first two sources should be carrying the load.

OHM's yield was 95%+ emissions. The Forge's yield is majority activity-based. That's the structural difference.

But the really important part isn't the yield. It's the loyalty multiplier.

When you stake in The Forge, your multiplier starts at 1x. Over six weeks, it climbs: 1.2x, 1.5x, 2.0x, 2.5x, 3.0x. Every week you stay, your share of the staking pool grows relative to newer stakers.

Unstake and it resets to 1x. Permanently. You don't get it back by restaking. You start over.

This is what OHM was missing. OHM had no cost to exit and re-enter. You could unstake, sell, wait for the bottom, and restake at the same terms as someone who held through the drawdown. There was no structural advantage to conviction.

In The Forge, conviction compounds. A staker at 3x earns three times the yield per token compared to a staker at 1x. That gap is permanent and irreversible. You can't buy your way back to it. You can only earn it by staying.

The covenant system adds another layer. When you stake, you choose a commitment level:

- Flame: 7-day lock, no bonus. Testing the water.
- Steel: 30-day lock, +50% APY. You've made a decision.
- Obsidian: 90-day lock, +150% APY. You're committed.
- Eternal: 365-day lock, +300% APY, no unstake. You understand something the others are still learning.

Your covenant isn't just a financial decision. It's a signal. In the forge, everyone can see what you committed to. Eternal holders carry weight that Flame holders don't. Not because of gatekeeping, but because the protocol mathematically values long-term conviction.

This is what we learned from studying communities like OHM, Frog Nation, and the LINK Marines. Identity and financial commitment reinforce each other. When someone locks Obsidian, they're declaring who they are in this ecosystem. They've chosen a side. That's the (3,3) energy, with economics underneath that are actually sustainable.

The burn mechanics close the loop. Every bout burns tokens — 10% of entry fees, 2% of every bet placed, and 5% of instant victory claims. At current parameters (20 entrants, 100K bet pool), that's ~3,000 from entries and bets plus claim burns on top. Three bouts a week. Burns scale with activity, not with a predetermined schedule. As participation grows, the burns grow proportionally.

OHM's supply expanded while it printed rewards. The Forge's supply contracts while it generates yield from real activity. One is reflexive on the way up and catastrophic on the way down. The other gets stronger as the ecosystem grows.

We're not pretending we invented (3,3). Zeus and the Olympus team did. What they built was one of the most important experiments in DeFi, and the community they created — the Ohmies — proved that game theory can drive genuine, passionate adoption. We studied that closely and we respect it.

What we did was fix the foundation. Keep the matrix. Keep the social dynamics. Keep the identity layer. Replace the yield source with something that doesn't depend on infinite growth.

The fire still rewards those who stay. We just made sure the fire is fed by work, not by printing.
